DIGITAL SECRETARIAT | DRAFT

2014 YEAR-END REPORT

Towards a sustainable and efficient organization

During the Joint Policy Committee and Management Committee decision 2011/9 on 28 April 2011 (items vi and ix), a decision provided the foundation for a more comprehensive vision of a digital Secretariat, aimed at creating a modern workplace with a high degree of digitization and virtualization of business workflows.

The result was inclusion of the Digital Secretariat initiative as a part of the global compact for each USG in the Secretariat. Each USG pledged the following:

I will implement agreed actions to reduce the United Nations’ carbon footprint and improve productivity, as per Secretary-General’s decision 2011/9.

The Digital Secretariat initiative focuses on improving sustainability and efficiency of the United Nations Secretariat. While specific goals have been defined to make our progress measurable, the effects of the efforts undertaken are expected to filter through many of our business processes.

The implementation and adoption of policies, processes, systems proposed by the initiative is an important driver of transformation within the Organization. The project aims at:

  • Reduction in paper usage and personal printers – facilitating progress in achieving a “greener” UN by saving paper, reduction in toner/ink consumption.
  • PaperSmart meeting technologies – focused on the reduction the carbon footprint of the Organization by using print-on-demand services, making meeting information and documents accessible.
  • ECM systems and record management policies – Standard Enterprise Content Management (ECM) systems and supporting retention schedules, file classification plans and security classifications. This is aimed at increasing the efficiency of administrative processes, improve the recording of business transactions, and make it easier to retrieve documents.
  • Social computing platforms, virtual, and mobile technologies – simplifying information access, facilitate online content optimised for mobile viewing in the six UN languages, flexible working arrangements, supporting business continuity and emergency preparedness, connecting and building internal communities where information is available and shared in real time.

A working group was formed of representatives from twenty-nine (29) offices, OAHs, and peace keeping missions within the Secretariat. The Office of Information and Communications Technology, assisted with the facilitation of this project starting 2014. A set of twelve (12) goals were monitored for the first time in 2014.

Twenty-nine (29) departments, offices, OAHs, and peace keeping operations in the Secretariat participated in this initiative, the majority of which made substantial progress. Their progress was monitored at the mid-year and at the end of the year.

The benefits realised in 2014 include process efficiency by streamlining document management and conference management, increased visibility to content, and data and time savings from the use of virtual meetings and mobile technologies. The introduction of new technologies and the changes to processes and policies will be of help to other projects that require policy changes and business process modification such as the Umoja implementation.

One important outcome is also a clear understanding of the challenges and opportunities for measuring progress in sustainability and efficiency across such a wide variety of departments and offices in the Secretariat in a standardized way. This has allowed us to formulate more specific, measurable, and relevant indicators for the Digital Secretariat initiative in 2015.

    Lessons learned and going forward

    The Digital Secretariat initiative has provided valuable lessons learned in 2014:

    1. Monitoring and reporting is hard: Many indicators could not be easily measured due to lack of available data in administrative systems. One of the major opportunities that the DS working group determined is in the future, indicators and methodologies should be chosen carefully. At the same time, better tools for tracking and monitoring data will help and, going forward, Umoja data will be an important source of information. Modern data analytics tools will also be of help to analyse and even forecast our progress.
    2. Goals should evolve over time: From the DS working group’s assessment preceding the 2014 Midyear Report, it was determined that several of the original goals have been achieved as much as possible (e.g. the elimination of personal printers), while new technologies and systems have opened up possibilities for new goals.
    3. Different offices benefit from different goals: The Secretariat is composed of a wide variety of entities, whose size, objectives, and mandates vary. In trying to formulate only goals that are meaningful to an individual office, potentially causes risk to not measure or evaluate meaningful goals for other offices.

    In light of these lessons, the working group decided to create four composite goals for 2015, addressing four main themes, each composed of mandatory goals for all offices and optional goals from which offices will select the ones most relevant to them. We have also adapted the methodologies to allow for more centralized, automated tracking. All in all, the expectation is that the resulting selection of goals for each office will be both more relevant and more easily measurable.

    New Goals for 2015

    With these observations in mind, the working group on the Digital Secretariat has formulated new goals for 2015. Four overarching composite goals have been defined, which consist of a mandatory component and optional sub-goals that departments, offices and OAHs will select from as appropriate for their particular situation. Together, these new goals will take us even further towards sustainability and efficiency. The Digital Secretariat can complement other projects: for instance the streamlining of business processes captured under the new “Smart Processes” composite goal will certainly facilitate the adoption of Umoja.
